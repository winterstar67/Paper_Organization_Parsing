First Determination of the Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation Temperature
at z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 Using Molecular Absorption Lines

Tatsuya Kotani School of Fundamental Science and Technology, Graduate School of Science and Technology, Keio University, 3-14-1 Hiyoshi, Kohoku-ku, Yokohama, Kanagawa 223-8522, Japan sci.tatsu.729@keio.jp Tomoharu Oka School of Fundamental Science and Technology, Graduate School of Science and Technology, Keio University, 3-14-1 Hiyoshi, Kohoku-ku, Yokohama, Kanagawa 223-8522, Japan Department of Physics, Faculty of Science and Technology, Keio University, 3-14-1 Hiyoshi, Kohoku-ku, Yokohama, Kanagawa 223-8522, Japan tomo@phys.keio.ac.jp Rei Enokiya National Astronomical Observatory of Japan, 2-21-1 Osawa, Mitaka, Tokyo 181-8588, Japan rei.enokiya@nao.ac.jp
(Accepted Feburary 24, 2026)
Abstract

We analyzed millimeter-wave data toward the quasar B0218+357 observed with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array and obtained absorption spectra of the JJ=2–1 and JJ=3–2 rotational transitions of HCN, HCO+, HNC, H13CN, and H13CO+ at the cosmological redshift of z=0.68z\!=\!0.68. For HCN, HCO+, and HNC, we identified two distinct absorption components that are common to both transitions, whereas a single component was detected in the isotopologue spectra. In this paper, we accurately evaluate the excitation temperatures and their uncertainties from the absorption strengths of these components, and use them to determine the CMB temperature. Uncertainties in the continuum covering factor were propagated into the excitation temperature via Monte Carlo sampling. We further corrected the observed optical depths for biases due to column-density nonuniformity by assuming a lognormal column-density distribution. Under the assumption that the rotational levels are in radiative equilibrium with the cosmic microwave background (CMB), we derived excitation temperature profiles in the optically thin regime. Because the excitation of HCO+ is biased by an additional velocity component and partial collisional excitation, this species was excluded from the final determination of the CMB temperature. From a weighted mean of the excitation temperatures obtained from HCN and HNC, we determined the CMB temperature at z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 to be 4.50±0.17​K{4.50\pm 0.17\,\mathrm{K}}. This constitutes the first measurement of the CMB temperature at z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 based on a quasar absorption line system and represents the most precise determination at this redshift, highly consistent with the standard Big Bang cosmological model.

I Introduction

The cosmic microwave background (CMB; Penzias & Wilson, 1965; Dicke et al., 1965) is a nearly uniform, isotropic blackbody radiation field with a current temperature of 2.72548±0.00057​K2.72548\pm 0.00057\,\mathrm{K} (Fixsen, 2009). It constitutes one of the strongest observational pillars of the Big Bang theory and is interpreted as a snapshot of the hot, dense, ionized Universe prior to the recombination epoch, approximately 3.8×1053.8\times 10^{5} years after the Big Bang. The CMB temperature (TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}}) decreases with the age of the Universe. In the standard Big Bang cosmology, it is predicted to evolve with the cosmic adiabatic expansion as,

TCMB​(z)=T0​(1+z),T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z)=T_{0}\,(1+z), (1)

where zz is the cosmological redshift and T0T_{0} is the present-day CMB temperature.

Equation (1) shows that the measurements of TCMB​(z)T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z) at multiple redshifts provide a direct test of cosmological models. Measurements of TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}} at z>0z\!>\!0 have been obtained by three principal methods. The first method is multifrequency thermal Sunyaev–Zel’dovich (SZ) observations (e.g., Battistelli et al., 2002; Horellou et al., 2005; Luzzi et al., 2009, 2015; Hurier et al., 2014; Saro et al., 2014; de Martino et al., 2015), which have yielded a large number of measurements primarily at z≲1z\!\lesssim\!1. The second involves analyses of H2O absorption lines against the CMB. This technique currently provides the highest-redshift measurement at z=6.34z\!=\!6.34 (Riechers et al., 2022); however, no other such samples have been identified to date, and the measurement carries a relatively large uncertainty (∼\sim30%). The third employs excitation analyses of atomic and molecular absorption lines observed toward quasars. Measurements based on electronic transition lines of species such as CO, [C I] or [C II] probe relatively high redshifts (2≲z≲32\lesssim{z}\lesssim 3), but their uncertainties are comparatively large (∼10\sim\!{10}–50%) (Ge et al., 1997; Srianand et al., 2000; Molaro et al., 2002; Cui et al., 2005; Klimenko et al., 2020). In contrast, rotational transition lines of highly polar molecules in intervening absorption systems—i.e., molecular clouds located in the foreground of the quasar—provide measurements at intermediate redshifts with much smaller fractional errors; for example, the measurement at z=0.89z\!=\!0.89 achieves a relative uncertainty of ∼\sim1–2% (Muller et al., 2013; Kotani et al., 2025). Although such systems are rare owing to the small molecular impact parameters required, they currently provide the most precise determinations of TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}} at z>0.5z>0.5. A recent analysis of the z=0.89z\!=\!0.89 absorber toward PKS1830–211 (Kotani et al., 2025) demonstrated that excitation analyses of highly polar molecules in diffuse molecular gas can yield high-precision measurements of TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}}, providing a methodological foundation for the present study. To date, all measurements obtained via these methods remain consistent with the predictions of the standard model. As illustrated in Figure 4 of Kotani et al. (2025), previous measurements of TCMB​(z)T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z) span a wide range of redshifts and methods, exhibiting sizable scatter—particularly at intermediate redshifts—despite being broadly consistent with the standard model prediction.

B0218+357 is a quasar at z=0.944z\!=\!0.944 (Cohen et al., 2003), and a nearly face-on spiral galaxy (York et al., 2005) is known to lie along its line of sight at z=0.68466z=0.68466 (Browne et al., 1993; Carilli et al., 1993; Wiklind & Combes, 1995). Due to the gravitational lensing produced by the intervening galaxy, the millimeter-wave image of B0218+357 is observed as two compact components on the sky, one located to the southwest and the other to the northeast, referred to as the A and B components, respectively (Jethava et al., 2007; Hada et al., 2020). The A and B components are offset from the center of the spiral galaxy in the lens plane by approximately 0.29 arcsec (2.0 kpc) and 0.05 arcsec (0.4 kpc), respectively (Hada et al., 2020). Along the line of sight toward the A component at z=0.68466z=0.68466, a rich molecular absorption system arising in the spiral galaxy has been detected, first discovered by Wiklind & Combes (1995). To date, numerous molecular species have been identified, including CO, CS, OH, C2H, HCN, HCO+, H2O, H2S, NH3, H2Cl+, and H2CO (Wiklind & Combes, 1995; Menten & Reid, 1996; Combes et al., 1997; Combes & Wiklind, 1997; Gerin et al., 1997; Kanekar et al., 2003; Henkel et al., 2005; Jethava et al., 2007; Muller et al., 2007; Zeiger & Darling, 2010; Kanekar, 2011; Wallström et al., 2016, 2019). Isotopologues detected in this system include 13CO, C34S, H13CN, HC15N, H13CO+, HC18O+, H342{}_{2}^{34}S, and H372{}_{2}^{37}Cl+ (Gerin et al., 1997; Wallström et al., 2016, 2019). This absorption system is one of few known intervening systems with rich molecular absorption features.

Both the A and B components of B0218+357 have not been previously used to measure the CMB temperature. However, Jethava et al. (2007) performed theoretical LVG calculations for the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber toward the A component, targeting CS, HCN, HCO+, HNC, and N2H+ and adopting physical conditions constrained by observations. They pointed out that, under optically thin conditions, the excitation temperatures of the rotational transitions of these molecules are expected to provide a reliable probe of TCMB​(z=0.68)T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z\!=\!0.68).

In this work, we report the first measurement of TCMB​(z=0.68)T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z\!=\!0.68) using quasar absorption lines, derived from millimeter data obtained with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) toward the A component of B0218+357. Using multiple highly polar molecules of HCN, HCO+, HNC and their isotopologues, we carried out an analysis that carefully accounts for uncertainties in the continuum covering factor and temporal variability of the absorption strength, and nonuniformity of the column density (Sect. III). By combining the results obtained for the individual molecular species, we derived a new, high-precision measurement of the CMB temperature at z=0.68z\!=\!0.68. This measurement, which probes the Universe roughly six billion years ago, places a tight constraint on deviations from the predictions of the standard Big Bang model (Sect. IV).

II Data Processing

We retrieved the ALMA Band 3 and Band 4 data toward B0218+357 from the ALMA Science Archive111https://almascience.eso.org/aq/ (Project code 2016.1.00031.S; PI: S. Muller). These observations contain the J=2–1 and J=3–2 absorption lines of HCN, HCO+, HNC, H13CN, and H13CO+, redshifted to z=0.68466z\!=\!0.68466.

Data calibration and reduction were carried out following the standard procedures in the Common Astronomy Software Applications (CASA) package222http://casa.nrao.edu/. The Band 3 data were obtained on 2017 May 2 and July 8, while the Band 4 data were obtained on 2016 October 7 and 23. For the 2017 May 2 dataset, quasar J0510+1800 was used as the bandpass calibrator and J0423–0120 as the flux calibrator, whereas J0237+2848 and J0238+1636 were served as the bandpass and flux calibrators, respectively, for the remaining datasets. Phase calibration was performed using quasar J0205+3212 for Band 3 and J0220+3241 for Band 4. Subsequently, using the CASA task mstransform, we extracted the spectral windows containing the HCN, HCO+, and HNC absorption lines with bandwidths of 0.23 GHz (0.47 GHz for the Band 4 data covering HNC), and the spectral windows containing the H13CN and H13CO+ absorption lines with bandwidths of 0.12 GHz (Band 3) and 0.94 GHz (Band 4), respectively.

The spectra toward components A and B of B0218+357 were extracted using the Python-based package UVMULTIFIT (Martí-Vidal et al., 2014) by directly fitting the visibilities for each component. We adopted a two-point-source model and obtained the spectra through a two-step procedure: (1) determining the peak positions using only the continuum visibilities, and (2) fitting the visibilities in each spectral channel at those positions. Although the two lensed images were not clearly resolved in the image plane, the simple and well-known two-point-source geometry of this system, with a separation of ∼0​.′′​3\!\sim\!0{\overset{\prime\prime}{.}}3 on the sky, enables spectral extraction via visibility fitting in the Fourier plane (e.g., Martí-Vidal et al., 2014; Wallström et al., 2019). In the following analysis, we use the spectrum toward the A component, which exhibits a richer set of strong molecular absorption lines.

The frequency axis of the extracted spectra was converted into radial velocity using the relativistic Doppler relationship V/c={1−(ν/ν0)2}/{1+(ν/ν0)2}V/c\!=\!\{1-({\nu}/{\nu_{0}})^{2}\}/\{1+({\nu}/{\nu_{0}})^{2}\}, where cc and ν0\nu_{0} denote the speed of light and rest frequency, respectively. The spectral axis was then regridded using the mstransform task to achieve the minimum velocity resolution specified in the ALMA archive, and the velocity reference frame was adopted as barycentric. The redshift of the absorption system (z=0.68466z\!=\!0.68466) corresponds to a systemic velocity of Vsys=1.4357×105V_{\mathrm{sys}}\!=\!1.4357\times 10^{5} km​s−1\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}. When constructing the absorption spectra, velocities are expressed as offsets relative to this VsysV_{\mathrm{sys}}.

III Analyses

In this study, we follow the analysis methodology presented in Kotani et al. (2025) to derive the optical depths and excitation temperatures from the molecular absorption line data, and to determine the CMB temperature. Section III.1 introduces the basis for deriving optical depth and excitation temperature. Section III.2 outlines the procedure for determining the CMB temperature from the excitation temperature. Section III.3 describes the sources of uncertainty in the excitation temperature, and Section III.4 summarizes the corrections applied to the optical depths.

III.1 Fundamentals

We assumed that the line of sight toward the quasar is obscured by a foreground, homogeneous absorbing cloud. The optical depth is computed from the continuum level (IcI_{\mathrm{c}}) and the absorption depth measured relative to it (Δ​I\Delta I) as

III.2 TCMBT_{\rm CMB} Calculations

The population of molecular rotational energy levels in the interstellar medium is governed by both collisional and radiative processes. In environments lacking local radiation fields and with extremely low densities of collision partners, such as diffuse gas, radiative coupling to the CMB becomes the dominant excitation mechanism. Because the relative contribution from collisional excitation scales with the number density of colliding particles, its importance can be assessed by comparing the gas density with the critical density. Molecules with larger electric dipole moments have correspondingly higher critical densities; for example, the critical densities of the HCN, HNC, and HCO+ J=1–0 transitions are of order ncrit​(H2)∼105−6​cm−3n_{\mathrm{crit}}({\rm H}_{2})\!\sim\!{10^{5-6}}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}} (Shirley, 2015), especially in the optically thin limit, and even higher values are required for higher-JJ transitions. Therefore, in the absence of bright local radiation sources, rotational transitions of highly polar molecules embedded in low-density diffuse gas are expected to be dominated by radiative excitation by the CMB.

As a possible local radiation field surrounding the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber toward the A component of B0218+357, thermal emission from dust may contribute. The total hydrogen column density (NHN_{\mathrm{H}}) is a excellent tracer of the dust mass column density (NdN_{\mathrm{d}}) (e.g., Bohlin et al., 1978), and their ratio, defined as δDG≡Nd/(mp​NH)\delta_{\mathrm{DG}}\equiv{N_{\mathrm{d}}/(m_{\mathrm{p}}N_{\mathrm{H}})} (Cheng et al., 2025), is known to be of order ∼0.01\sim 0.01 in the Milky Way (Draine & Hensley, 2021), where mpm_{\mathrm{p}} is the proton mass. Accordingly, the dust optical depth (τd\tau_{\mathrm{d}}) can be expressed using the mass absorption coefficient (κν\kappa_{\nu}) as

τd=κν​Nd=κν​δDG​mp​NH.\tau_{\mathrm{d}}=\kappa_{\nu}{N_{\mathrm{d}}}=\kappa_{\nu}{\delta_{\mathrm{DG}}m_{\mathrm{p}}N_{\mathrm{H}}}. (4)

Assuming that NHN_{\mathrm{H}} toward the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber is given by the sum of NN(H\mathrm{H}\,I) =5×1020​cm−2\!=\!5\times 10^{20}\,\mathrm{cm^{-2}} and N​(H2)=N({\rm H}_{2})\!=(0.1–2)×1022​cm−2\times 10^{22}\,{\rm cm}^{-2}, as estimated by Henkel et al. (2005), the dust optical depth at the relevant frequencies is calculated to be τd∼10−7\tau_{\mathrm{d}}\sim 10^{-7}–10−610^{-6}. Adopting this value of τd\tau_{\mathrm{d}} together with a typical dust temperature at the solar position in the Milky Way, Td=23​KT_{\mathrm{d}}=23\,\mathrm{K} (Wright et al., 1991), we compare the intensities of the dust thermal emission (IdI_{\mathrm{d}}) and the CMB radiation (ICMBI_{\mathrm{CMB}}) at z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 using

IdICMB=Bν​(Td)​(1−e−τd)Bν​(TCMB​(z)),\frac{I_{\mathrm{d}}}{I_{\mathrm{CMB}}}=\frac{B_{\nu}(T_{\mathrm{d}})(1-e^{-\tau_{\mathrm{d}}})}{B_{\nu}(T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z))}, (5)

where Bν​(T)B_{\nu}(T) is the Planck function. The resulting ratio is found to be ∼10−6\sim 10^{-6}–10−510^{-5}. Thus, the dust radiation field is negligible compared to the CMB, and its contribution to the rotational excitation of the molecules considered in this study can be safely neglected in the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorption system.

The gas density and kinetic temperature of the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber have been estimated to be n​(H2)∼102−4​cm−3n(\mathrm{H_{2}})\!\sim\!{10^{2-4}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}} (Henkel et al., 2005; Jethava et al., 2007; Zeiger & Darling, 2010) and Tkin=55​KT_{\mathrm{kin}}\!=\!{55\,\mathrm{K}} (Henkel et al., 2005), respectively, suggesting that the absorbing medium consists predominantly of diffuse molecular gas (e.g., Henkel et al., 2005; Jethava et al., 2007). Under these conditions, the rotational level populations of the highly polar molecules detected in this absorber, namely HCN, HCO+, HNC, and their isotopologues, are in radiative equilibrium with the CMB. Therefore, in this regime the relation

TCMB=TexT_{\mathrm{CMB}}=T_{\mathrm{ex}} (6)

holds. Indeed, the excitation analysis by Wallström et al. (2016) assumes that collisional excitation is negligible compared to radiative excitation by the CMB. In addition, previous TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}} measurements toward the SW component of PKS1830–211 at zabs=0.89z_{\mathrm{abs}}=0.89 likewise relied on this relation, using absorption lines of highly polar molecules arising in diffuse molecular gas (Wiklind & Combes, 1996; Menten et al., 1999; Kotani et al., 2025). We also use this relation in our analysis.

III.3 Uncertainty

III.3.1 Continuum Covering Factor

An incorrect assumption for fcf_{\mathrm{c}} directly affects the inferred optical depth and can lead to a biased estimate of the excitation temperature. For the A component, Muller et al. (2007) estimated fc≥0.77f_{\mathrm{c}}\geq 0.77 from the saturated absorption bottom of the ∼5​km​s−1\sim 5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component of the HCO+ JJ=2–1 line. However, a consensus value for fcf_{\mathrm{c}} at millimeter wavelengths has not yet been established: Wallström et al. (2016) adopted 0.8, whereas some study assumed fc=1f_{\mathrm{c}}\!=\!1 (Wiklind & Combes, 1995; Jethava et al., 2007; Zeiger & Darling, 2010; Wallström et al., 2019). Moreover, Jethava et al. (2007) pointed out that fcf_{\mathrm{c}} may vary with frequency. These uncertainties in fcf_{\mathrm{c}} represent a major source of error in the derived excitation temperature.

To rigorously account for both the indeterminacy and the possible frequency dependence of fcf_{\mathrm{c}}, we performed Monte Carlo calculations. We assumed that fcf_{\mathrm{c}} follows a uniform distribution between 0.77 and 1.00. This choice reflects the weak physical constraints on fcf_{\mathrm{c}} and thus adopts a maximally noninformative prior. The absorption depth normalized by the continuum level, Δ​I/Ic=1−Iobs{\Delta I/I_{\mathrm{c}}}=1-I_{\mathrm{obs}}, is assumed to follow a normal distribution, with its mean and standard deviation given by the observed value and its uncertainty, respectively. Here, IobsI_{\mathrm{obs}} denotes the observed, continuum-normalized absorption spectrum at each velocity channel. For each velocity bin and each transition, we drew random and independent samples of fcf_{\mathrm{c}} and Δ​I/Ic\Delta I/I_{\mathrm{c}} from their respective distributions and computed the optical depth using Equation (2). Repeating this procedure 100,000 times yielded the probability distribution of the optical depth. We adopted the median and the 1σ\sigma confidence interval of this distribution as the observed optical depth (τobs\tau_{\mathrm{obs}}) and its uncertainty. In this way, the uncertainty in fcf_{\mathrm{c}}, including its possible time and frequency dependence, was fully propagated, together with the statistical uncertainty in IobsI_{\mathrm{obs}}, into the final estimate of τobs\tau_{\mathrm{obs}}.

III.3.2 Time Variability

Monitoring observations of the HCN and HCO+ JJ=2–1 absorption toward B0218+357 with the IRAM Plateau de Bure Interferometer (PdBI) over a three-year period (2005–2008) revealed that the spectra do not exhibit absorption variations down to a few percent of the total continuum level of the combined A and B images (Wallström et al., 2016). No millimeter monitoring observations have been carried out since this observation. The Band 3 and Band 4 observations used in this study are separated by approximately nine months (from October 2016 to July 2017), but no flares, rapid millimeter-wavelength activity, or changes in the background quasar’s continuum morphology have been reported during this interval. On the basis of these considerations, we assume that no significant variability in the absorption strength occurred over this short period. Even if variability at the level of a few percent is assumed toward the A image alone in this analysis, the resulting change in the inferred CMB temperature is less than 0.1 K, which is well within the final statistical uncertainty.

III.4 Correction for Nonuniformity

In Equation (2), we assumed that the absorbing cloud has a uniform column density and partially covers the background quasar. In reality, however, the column density of the cloud is nonuniform. Regions with higher column density saturate the observed absorption depth Δ​I/Ic\Delta I/I_{\mathrm{c}}, leading to an underestimation of τν\tau_{\nu} and NJN_{J}. The effect of this column-density nonuniformity becomes more significant at larger optical depths and results in an overestimation of TexT_{\rm ex}.

Kotani et al. (2025) corrected this bias by assuming that the column density follows a lognormal probability distribution function (N-PDF) and by computing a correction factor relating the observed optical depth (τobs\tau_{\rm obs}) to the averaged actual optical depth (τreal\tau_{\rm real}). The mean column density of the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber toward the A component of B0218+357 is constrained to be N​(H2)=N({\rm H}_{2})\!=(0.1–2)×1022​cm−2\times 10^{22}\,{\rm cm}^{-2} (Henkel et al., 2005; Wallström et al., 2016, 2019). In this study, we adopted the N-PDF parameters derived by Ma et al. (2020) for the Orion A giant molecular cloud, which has a comparable mean column density of ∼1.5×1022​cm−2\sim 1.5\times 10^{22}\,{\rm cm}^{-2} (Ma et al., 2020), to compute the correction. This choice is further justified because lognormal N-PDFs are ubiquitously observed in Galactic molecular clouds, and observations also support lognormal-like distributions in low-density, extragalactic environments. The resulting relation between τobs\tau_{\rm obs} and τreal\tau_{\rm real} is shown in Figure 1 of Kotani et al. (2025). We applied this nonuniformity correction to all data points in the absorption spectra.

IV Results and Discussion

IV.1 Optical Depth

Figure 1 presents the normalized absorption spectra of the HCN, HCO+, HNC, H13CN, and H13CO+ J=2–1 and J=3–2 transitions detected toward B0218+357 A. The HC15N J=2–1 and J=3–2 transitions also fall within the observed spectral windows, but neither line is detected. In the absorption profiles of HCN, HCO+, and HNC, at least two components are commonly observed, centered at Vbar=+5​km​s−1V_{\rm bar}\!=\!+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} and −6​km​s−1-6\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}, respectively. These two components correspond to the c and a components reported by Muller et al. (2007), or to the first and third components described by Wallström et al. (2016). For both HCN and HCO+, the third absorption component, centered near Vbar=0​km​s−1V_{\rm bar}\!=\!0\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}, is present in the J=3–2 transition for HCN, whereas it is seen in both transitions for HCO+. This feature is likely correspond to the b component identified by Muller et al. (2007). The +5​km​s−1+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component is also visible in the profiles of H13CN and H13CO+, whereas the −6​km​s−1-6\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component is not detected in either species. Compared with the other molecules and transitions, the HCN J=2–1 line appears to be saturated at the bottom of the +5​km​s−1+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component. This behavior is consistent with the PdBI results reported by Wallström et al. (2016). If we assume that the optical depth becomes infinite at the absorption minimum, Equation (2) yields a lower limit of fc∼0.8f_{\rm c}\sim 0.8, which is consistent with the constraint fc≥0.77f_{\rm c}\geq 0.77.

The top panel of Figure 2 shows the optical depth profiles of HCN, HCO+, and HNC after accounting for the uncertainty in fcf_{\rm c} and correcting for the bias introduced by the nonuniform column-density structure. Both HCN and HCO+ are optically thick at the absorption peak of the +5​km​s−1+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component in both transitions. For HNC, only the J=2–1 transition is optically thick at the peak of the +5​km​s−1+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component. In contrast, the H13CN and H13CO+ lines are optically thin in both transitions, with τmax∼0.10\tau_{\rm max}\!\sim\!0.10–0.15 for the J=2–1 lines and τmax∼0.04\tau_{\rm max}\!\sim\!0.04–0.05 for the J=3–2 lines.

IV.2 Excitation Temperature

In this study, following Kotani et al. (2025), we evaluated the optical depth on a per-velocity-channel basis for the high signal-to-noise ratio (S/N), optically thick HCN, HCO+, and HNC lines. We then derived the excitation temperature by computing a weighted average of the resulting TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} values. For the low-S/N (∼3\sim 3), optically thin H13CN and H13CO+ lines, we derived the excitation temperature using the optical depths integrated over the full velocity ranges exhibiting absorption.

IV.2.1 HCN, HCO+, and HNC

All spectra of these molecules were resampled to match the velocity spacing of the J=3–2 data, which have the lowest spectral resolution (Δ​Vbar=0.909​km​s−1\Delta V_{\mathrm{bar}}=0.909\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}). TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} and NN were determined by performing a weighted least-squares fit of Equation (III.1) to the integrated optical depth calculated in each velocity bin. The initial value of TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} was set to the standard Big Bang model prediction, TCMB​(z=0.68)=4.59​KT_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z=0.68)=4.59\,\mathrm{K}. For the initial column densities, we adopted 7.8×1013​cm−27.8\times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{cm^{-2}} and 4.5×1013​cm−24.5\times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{cm^{-2}} for HCN and HCO+, respectively, as reported by Wiklind & Combes (1995). For the HNC column density, an initial value of 2.6×1013​cm−22.6\times 10^{13}\,\mathrm{cm^{-2}} was adopted. Because no prior studies have analyzed the HNC line toward this source, we estimated the initial value using the abundance ratio N​(HNC)/N​(HCN)N(\mathrm{HNC})/N(\mathrm{HCN}) measured in the PKS1830–211 absorber (Muller et al., 2011, 2016), which exhibits absorption from similar diffuse molecular gas, together with the HCN column density of Wiklind & Combes (1995). These values were used to calculate the column density per velocity bin. To ensure that the derived excitation temperature reliably reflects the CMB temperature, the analysis was restricted to the velocity ranges where the optical depth is less than unity. In this regime, photon trapping and collisional excitation are expected to be negligible, and Equation (6) is therefore applicable.

The lower panels of Figure 2 show the derived excitation temperature profiles. The uncertainty in TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} corresponds to the 68% confidence interval of the Monte Carlo-derived distribution. Based on these profiles, we focused on the regions associated with the two velocity components. For the +5​km​s−1+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component, the HCN JJ=2–1 absorption line appears to be saturated at its absorption center, allowing us to place a constraint on fcf_{\mathrm{c}} that is consistent with previous studies. In contrast, the −6​km​s−1-6\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component shows no evidence of saturation, preventing a meaningful constraint on fcf_{\mathrm{c}} in that velocity range; consequently, the associated TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} remains uncertain. For this reason, we consider the excitation temperature of the +5​km​s−1+5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} component to provide the most reliable estimate of the CMB temperature. We therefore computed, for each molecule, the weighted mean excitation temperature within the velocity range given in Table 1, using

To quantitatively assess the impact of collisional excitation, we performed RADEX (van der Tak et al., 2007) calculations adopting the physical conditions of the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorbing cloud (Tkin=55​KT_{\mathrm{kin}}\!=\!55\,\mathrm{K} and n​(H2)=5×103​cm−3n(\mathrm{H_{2}})\!=\!5\times 10^{3}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}) reported by Henkel et al. (2005). In this calculation, only the CMB was assumed as the radiation field. The line widths were set to approximately 17 km​s−1\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}, 17 km​s−1\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}, and 10 km​s−1\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} for HCN, HCO+, and HNC, respectively, based on the spectra reported in Wiklind & Combes (1995). The resulting excitation temperatures are approximately 4.7 K, 5.0 K, and 4.7 K for HCN, HCO+, and HNC, respectively. For HCN, the ⟨Tex⟩\langle T_{\rm ex}\rangle value listed in Table 1 is lower than the corresponding value calculated with RADEX. This suggests that, under the physical conditions of the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber, collisional excitation plays a negligible role in populating the rotational levels of HCN. For HNC, under the adopted physical conditions, the excitation temperature derived from RADEX is comparable to our measured value. However, if a higher gas density of n​(H2)∼104​cm−3n(\mathrm{H_{2}})\!\sim\!10^{4}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}} is assumed, as reported by Zeiger & Darling (2010), the RADEX calculations yield an excitation temperature that is higher than the value derived in our analysis. These results indicate that, for both HCN and HNC in this absorber, the rotational excitation is governed predominantly by radiative excitation from the CMB rather than by collisional excitation.

In contrast, even after accounting for uncertainties, the excitation temperature derived from the profile analysis for HCO+ exceeds the RADEX calculations. Based on approximate estimates inferred from the results of Shirley (2015), the critical densities of the JJ=2–1 and JJ=3–2 transitions of HCN, HCO+, and HNC at Tkin=55​KT_{\rm kin}=55\,\mathrm{K} are (2–6)×106​cm−3\times 10^{6}\,{\rm cm^{-3}}, (0.3–1)×106​cm−3\times 10^{6}\,{\rm cm^{-3}}, and (0.8–3)×106​cm−3\times 10^{6}\,{\rm cm^{-3}}, respectively, in the optically thin regime. The excitation temperatures exhibit an inverse dependence on the critical densities, in that species with higher critical densities have lower excitation temperatures. This indicates that the excitation-temperature behavior of HCO+ found in our analysis is likely caused by the lower critical densities of the rotational levels of HCO+ compared to those of HCN and HNC, which makes HCO+ more susceptible to collisional excitation. An alternative possibility is contamination from the third velocity component near Vbar=0​km​s−1V_{\rm bar}\!=\!0\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}, which appears to be in a higher excitation state and could bias the derived temperatures high. Indeed, this component is clearly confirmed in both the JJ=2–1 and JJ=3–2 transitions of HCO+, and its excitation temperatures decrease systematically toward the line wings over the velocity range Vbar=−2.7V_{\rm bar}\!=\!-2.7 to +1.0​km​s−1+1.0\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}. Therefore, the value of ⟨Tex⟩\langle T_{\mathrm{ex}}\rangle derived for HCO+ may not provide an accurate estimate of the CMB temperature.

For this reason, we regard the HCN and HNC results as the most reliable tracers of the CMB temperature in the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber toward the A image of B0218+357. We then applied the weighted-mean procedure described by Equation (7) to the excitation temperatures of these two species. Under the relation expressed in Equation (6), we obtain

TCMB​(z=0.68)=4.50±0.17​K.T_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z\!=\!0.68)=4.50\pm 0.17\,\mathrm{K}. (8)

This value is in good agreement, within the uncertainties, with the prediction of the standard Big Bang model (4.59​K4.59~\mathrm{K}). Compared with previous measurements at z∼0.68z\!\sim\!0.68 based on SZ observations, which yielded 4.78±0.28​K4.78\pm 0.28~\mathrm{K} at z=0.676z\!=\!0.676 (Hurier et al., 2014) and 4.72−0.27+0.39​K4.72^{+0.39}_{-0.27}~\mathrm{K} at z=0.681z\!=\!0.681 (Saro et al., 2014), our result provides a more precise determination.

IV.2.2 13C isotopologues

The absorption spectra of H13CN and H13CO+ have relatively low S/N. We therefore fitted each optical depth profile with a single Gaussian component and computed the respective velocity-integrated optical depths over the range −5​km​s−1-5\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} to +15​km​s−1+15\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} for each transition of each molecule. TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} and NN were then determined by performing a weighted least-squares fit using Equation (III.1). The initial value of TexT_{\mathrm{ex}} was set to the standard model prediction, as in Section IV.2.1. The initial values of NN were adopted from Wallström et al. (2016), namely 2.9×1012​cm−22.9\times 10^{12}\,\mathrm{cm^{-2}} for H13CN and 2.0×1012​cm−22.0\times 10^{12}\,\mathrm{cm^{-2}} for H13CO+. The resulting excitation temperatures are listed in Table 1. Although these uncertainties are larger than those derived for HCN and HNC, both values are consistent within uncertainties with the standard-model prediction of 4.59​K4.59\,\mathrm{K}.

For these molecules as well, we performed RADEX calculations adopting the same physical conditions as those applied in Section IV.2.1, together with line widths of approximately 6.3 km​s−1\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} and 4.5 km​s−1\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}} for H13CN and H13CO+, respectively (Wallström et al., 2016). The resulting excitation temperatures are ∼4.7​K\sim 4.7\,\mathrm{K} and ∼5.0​K\sim 5.0\,\mathrm{K} for H13CN and H13CO+, respectively. The excitation temperature derived from the absorption profile is lower than that obtained from RADEX calculations for H13CO+, suggesting that its rotational levels are in radiative equilibrium with the CMB. However, because the absorption is weak and the S/N is low for these isotopologues, we did not use them in the final estimate of the CMB temperature.

V Summary and Future Prospects

In this study, we analyzed molecular absorption lines detected in ALMA millimeter-wave observations toward the quasar B0218+357 and obtained the most precise measurement to date of the CMB temperature at z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 independent of SZ observations. We extracted absorption spectra of the J=2–1 and J=3–2 transitions of HCN, HCO+, HNC, H13CN, and H13CO+, and derived the excitation temperature TexT_{\rm ex} and its associated uncertainty. The impact of the uncertainty in the continuum covering factor (fcf_{\rm c}) was evaluated using a Monte Carlo approach over the expected range of fcf_{\rm c}. Biases in the observed optical depths arising from inhomogeneities in the absorbing cloud were corrected by assuming a lognormal probability distribution for the column density. Taking into account the physical conditions of the absorber, we computed the weighted mean of the excitation temperatures derived from HCN and HNC, yielding TCMB​(z=0.68)=4.50±0.17​KT_{\mathrm{CMB}}(z\!=\!0.68)\!=\!4.50\pm 0.17\,\mathrm{K}. This value is in good agreement with the prediction of the standard Big Bang model and places constraints on nonstandard cosmological scenarios. These constraints are consistent with those obtained in previous studies. The excitation temperatures derived from H13CN and H13CO+ are also consistent with the prediction of the standard Big Bang model, although their statistical significance is limited by the lower S/N of the data.

Obtaining high-S/N absorption lines of isotopologues of HCN, HCO+, and HNC remains an important goal for achieving more robust estimates of the CMB temperature. Owing to their high critical densities, the excitation temperatures of their rotational levels are expected to serve as reliable tracers of the CMB temperature in the z=0.68z\!=\!0.68 absorber toward B0218+357. Achieving this will require highly sensitive spectral-line observations.

For a more stringent test of the standard Big Bang cosmological model, it is also essential to increase the number of high-precision measurements of TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}} at z>1z>1. A natural extension of this work will be ALMA observations toward quasars hosting absorbers at z=2​–​3z\!=\!2\mbox{--}3 along the line of sight, targeting highly polar molecular lines. In addition, although they are relatively sensitive to collisional excitation, millimeter-wave fine-structure transitions of neutral carbon ([C I]) also constitute useful targets. Compared to highly polar molecules, [C I] is more widely distributed in the interstellar medium, allowing for a larger sample of high-redshift absorption systems. Indeed, analyses of [C I] absorption lines in the optical regime have already provided a number of measurements of the CMB temperature at high redshifts (z≳2z\gtrsim 2) (e.g., Klimenko et al., 2020). Future radio facilities, such as the Square Kilometre Array, and the upgraded ALMA, are expected to make significant contributions by increasing both the number and precision of TCMBT_{\mathrm{CMB}} measurements at high redshift (z>1z\!>\!1). This progress will enable increasingly sensitive tests with small deviations from the standard cosmological model.
